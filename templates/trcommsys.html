The transmitting and receiving of data and information is done through communication concepts and completed by network hardware and software.
Protocols and handshaking
As established in characteristics of communication systems, protocols are a set of rules that regulates transfer of data between computers.
They define how the devices establish a link, how data is transmitted and the method for error detection and correction.
Using the same protocol means that different computers and devices can all communicate with each other.
The most comon protocols are:

  1. TCP/IP (Transmission Control Protocol/Internet Protocol), which is TCP and IP working together to regulate data transmission and error detection.
  2. HTTP (Hypertext Transfer Protocol): A protocol that allows users to send and retrieve files over the internet. Also allows access to web pages.
  3. FTP (File Transfer Protocol): Enables a computer to connect remotely to a server. Mostly used for remote file access.
  4. POP (Post Office Protocol): A protocol which specifies how email messages may be exchanged between a computer and the ISP.
Davis, S. Information Processes and Technology - The HSC Course. P89. Published 2004
Networks
A network is a number of computers connected together. Each device in a network is called a node.
Terminals are devices that send and receive data from another computer.
Networks are classified in two ways:
  1. Local area networks: Connect devices within a building or campus together, and is linked with coaxial cable or fibre-optic cable.
  2. Wide area networks: Connect devices over long distances. They often consist of a mainframe computer called the host and a number of terminals.
Network topologies
There are many possible types of network topologies. The most common ones are star, bus and ring.
  1. A star topology has a central computer with each device connected directly to it.
  2. A bus topology is an arrangement where all devices are attached to a direct line called the bus.
  3. A ring topology is an arrancement where all devices are attached so that the path of in the shape of a continuous circle. Data flows only in one direction until data reaches its destination in this topology.

Transmission media
Data is transferred along a transmission medium, or hardware which allows data to pass through such as cables.
Transmission media are either wired or wireless:
Wire transmission transfers the data through wires and cables. Advantages of using cables are that they have high bandwidth with little interferance. Disadvantages include: Taking up physical space, difficult to install and must be protected.
Examples of wire transmission media include:
  1. Twisted-pair cables: Consisting of two thin insulated copper wires which are twisted to form a spiral.
  2. Coaxial cable (also known as coax): which is a single copper wire surrounded by an insulator, ground shielding and an outer insulator.
  3. Fibre-optic cable: Uses a light to carry data in small glass fibres. Fibre-optic is free from electromagnetic and radio interference, is very secure and can transmit data at very high speeds with few errors. However, they are fragile and easy to damage.

[Wire transmission] Powers, G.K. Information Processes and Technology HSC Course. P95 Published 2000.

Wireless transmission moves data through the air without the need of any sort of physical connection.
Examples of wireless transmission are radio and television.
Wireless transmission involves:
  1. A microwave, which is a high-frequency radio sent in a straight line from one antenna to another. Microwave transmission is faster than telephone lines and coaxial cables, however the transmission can be affected by obstacles such as weather, trees and buildings.
  2. Satellites, which is a receiver and transmitter that is launched by a rocket and placed in orbit around the earth. Signals are sent from the ground to the statellite, where the signal is sent to another place on the ground.
  3. Wireless LANs use radio waves as their transmission medium. Concerns about electromagnetic radiation have made it hard to popularise wireless networks.
  4. Mobile phonestransmit data to a grid of cellular stations that are linked to the wire-transmission telephone network.
